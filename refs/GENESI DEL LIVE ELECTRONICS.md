## 1. ORIGINE DEL TERMINE "LIVE ELECTRONICS"

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Il termine viene coniato da John Cage nel 1962

**CITAZIONE 1 - Di Scipio, *Thinking Liveness* (2021):**
> "It is usually agreed (e.g., Sallis et al. 2018, 1) that the expression live electronic music was coined by John Cage in 1962 (with reference to his Cartridge Music, 1960)"

**CITAZIONE 2 - Di Scipio, *Circuiti del tempo* [3.5]:**
> "La possibilità di gestire mezzi elettronici ed elettroacustici in concerto — 'dal vivo' — non nasce certo negli anni 1960: i molteplici strumenti di liuteria elettronica della prima metà del '900 erano ovviamente destinati a tale impiego, essendo quasi sempre stati concepiti appunto come strumenti musicali. Cosa si intende allora con *live electronics* — cioè con 'elettronica dal vivo' — e perché parlare di 'esordi' in riferimento a circostanze che hanno avuto luogo negli anni 1960?"

**CITAZIONE 3 - Di Scipio, *Circuiti del tempo* [3.5]:**
> "Va comunque considerato che **la locuzione 'live electronic music' è stata coniata nei primi anni 1960 (sarebbe stato John Cage a farlo)**, e che inizialmente venne spesso collegata a collettivi musicali dediti a pratiche improvvisative — come il collettivo basato a Roma e denominato non a caso Musica Elettronica Viva [3.5.6]."

**CITAZIONE 4 - Di Scipio, *Circuiti del tempo* (Parte Terza, immagine da Bernardini):**
> "Come anticipato, fu proprio parlando di Cartridge Music che Cage avrebbe usato l'espressione «musica elettronica dal vivo» forse per la prima volta in assoluto (ciò avvenne comunque due anni dopo la composizione, nel 1962)."

**CITAZIONE 5 - Di Scipio, *Thinking Liveness* (2021):**
> "those earliest endeavours aimed to take electronically generated music 'out of the studio' (Chadabe 1997, 81) and to 'animate' (Pousseur 1976, 243) creative designs that were otherwise limited to fixed audio media (tape music)"

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Definizione operativa - antitesi alla tape music

**CITAZIONE 6 - Di Scipio, *Circuiti del tempo* [3.5.1]:**
> "Si parla di musica *live electronics* **fondamentalmente per antitesi rispetto a repertori di musica elettroacustica interamente realizzata 'in studio' (fissata su supporto, riprodotta in situazioni di fruizione acusmatica)**. Più precisamente, la locuzione implica che *produzione e articolazione dei suoni hanno luogo al momento dell'esecuzione musicale* con dispositivi elettroacustici *assemblati o anche messi a punto secondo un piano originale*, cioè secondo un piano ideato per eseguire un certo specifico brano o per dar luogo a una certa prassi esecutiva."

**CITAZIONE 7 - Di Scipio, *Circuiti del tempo* [3.5.1]:**
> "Le condizioni essenziali sono quindi due: si tratta di mettere in campo dispositivi — **funzionanti e controllati 'dal vivo' (*live* in inglese, *en direct* in francese), senza ricorrere a suoni preregistrati**"

**CITAZIONE 8 - Chadabe, *Electric Sound* - Capitolo "Out of the Studios":**
> "Question: 'How do you perform electronic music without tape?' Answer (said with a shrug of the shoulders and rising inflection): 'Take away the tape.'"

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Distinzione LIVE ≠ REAL-TIME

**CITAZIONE 9 - Di Scipio, *Circuiti del tempo* [4.3]:**
> "Ci volgeremo ora verso esperienze in cui sistemi informatici **operanti 'in tempo reale'** sono stati messi in azione anche **'dal vivo'**, cioè in concerto o in altri contesti performativi."

**CITAZIONE 10 - Di Scipio, *Thinking Liveness* (2021):**
> "I should emphasise that live is not the same as real time (Emmerson 1994, 2012a). Computer music institutions have always customarily spoken of 'real-time computers' rather than 'live electronics,' assuming the technologically-determined criterion of higher and higher computational speed"

**CITAZIONE 11 - Bernardini, "On the emergence of live electronics":**
> "Live electronics behaves like an instrument: it produces output, in real time, in a causal relationship between a generating gesture (which may be the press of a button or an incoming sound event). This is completely different from the previous electronic technologies (i.e. tape recorders and magnetic tape in general): these were much closer to a composing tool – being non–causal and not imposing any restriction on sound manipulation."

**[NOTA: Questa citazione mostra che Di Scipio usa "tempo reale" e "dal vivo" come concetti DISTINTI - il primo riguarda operazioni della macchina, il secondo la condizione performativa]**

## 2. IMAGINARY LANDSCAPE SERIES (PRECORRITORI)

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Prime esplorazioni elettroniche di Cage

**CITAZIONE 12 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 169):**
> "Cage's first significant music using electronic technology was the series Imaginary Landscapes, beginning with Imaginary Landscape No. 1 (1939). The instruments included two variable-speed phonographs used to perform with radio sound-testing records, a large Chinese cymbal, and a 'string piano' in the manner of Henry Cowell."

**CITAZIONE 13 - Collins, *Handmade Electronic Music*:**
> "**Imaginary Landscape No. 1, composed in 1939 for piano, bowed cymbal, and record player, was the first documented piece of music to feature the DJ as a musical performer**, while **Imaginary Landscape No. 4 (1951) was composed for a dozen ordinary radios**."

**CITAZIONE 14 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 169):**
> "Imaginary Landscape No. 2 of 1942 was Cage's first use of amplified small sounds. A resonating coil of wire was attached to a phonograph cartridge and amplified, as a practical replacement for the large metal gong that Lora Deja had taken with her when she left Seattle for New York."

**CITAZIONE 15 - Di Scipio, *Circuiti del Tempo* (immagine da pagina Bernardini):**
> "Un discorso simile si può fare per Imaginary Landscape n.4 (un brano del 1951 eseguito dal vivo con dodici ricevitori radiofonici gestiti da ventiquattro esecutori) e per Radio Music (con un numero variabile di esecutori — fino a otto — altrettanti ricevitori radiofonici, 1956)."

## 3. CARTRIDGE MUSIC (1960) - SVOLTA STORICA

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Microsounds amplificati come alternativa agli studios europei

**CITAZIONE 16 - Collins, *Handmade Electronic Music* (p. 1561):**
> "In Cartridge Music (1960), performers substitute springs, twigs, pipe cleaners, and other thin objects for the needles in cheap record player pickups; **the surprising richness of these enormously amplified 'microsounds' rivaled the more labor- (and capital-)intensive synthetic sonorities coming out of the European electronic music studios** and opened the ears of a generation of sound artists to the splendor of the contact mike."

**CITAZIONE 17 - Collins, *Handmade Electronic Music* (Art & Music 2):**
> "The influence of John Cage (1912–1992) on American avant-garde music cannot be overstated. Given the breadth of his impact as a composer and theoretician, **his significance in the rise of hacker electronic culture is sometimes overlooked.**"

**CITAZIONE 18 - Collins, citando Fantechi:**
> "Its possibilities **dislodged mid-century European composition from its commitment to tape music and sparked an intercontinental fascination with live electronic music**"

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Tecnica e materiali utilizzati

**CITAZIONE 19 - Chadabe, *Electric Sound* (p. 81-82):**
> "The title Cartridge Music derives from the use in its performance of cartridges, that is, phonograph pick-ups into which needles are inserted for playing recordings. Contact microphones are also used. These latter are applied to chairs, tables, wastebaskets, etc.: **various suitable objects (toothpicks, matches, slinkies, piano wires, feathers, etc.) are inserted in the cartridges**. Both the microphones and cartridges are connected to amplifiers that go to loud-speakers, **the majority of the sounds produced being small and requiring amplification in order to be heard**..."

**CITAZIONE 20 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 20):**
> "By the late 1950s Cage had Tudor searching for a variety of electronic transducers, which were then becoming commonplace in the development of **solid-state transistor technology**. These transducers became part of the instrumental 'ensemble' for Cage's iconic **Cartridge Music of 1960**. By this time, Cage had already composed several works using **amplified 'small sounds'**, often performed with Tudor as pianist. These small sounds, usually inaudible to an audience without amplification, were derived from live acoustical sources (piano, percussion, invented or found instruments)."

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Approccio profetico all'amplificazione

**CITAZIONE 21 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts*:**
> "Cage's use of amplification was prophetic: instead of amplifying sounds that were simply not quite loud enough, he magnified micro-sounds that were practically inaudible without amplification, revealing a whole new world of sound resources."

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Approccio DIY e filosofia

**CITAZIONE 22 - Collins, *Handmade Electronic Music*:**
> "Throughout his career, Cage had a passionate curiosity for new sounds and compositional strategies. **Lacking institutional support in the way of orchestral commissions and the like, Cage, the son of an inventor, chose to develop new instruments from everyday technology and commonplace objects.**"

**CITAZIONE 23 - Collins, *Handmade Electronic Music*:**
> "At the beginning of his career, he literally made do with rubbish: his early percussion music, such as First Construction in Metal (1939), **used brake drums and other scrap iron from junkyards**."

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Score e open-form structure

**CITAZIONE 24 - Chadabe, *Electric Sound* (p. 82):**
> "Cage's score to Cartridge Music, like his score to Fontana Mix, **consists of graphic materials on transparent plastic sheets which a performer can overlay in different juxtapositions to devise a specific structure for a particular performance**. Most of Cage's scores from the period—for example, Fontana Mix (1958), Variations I (1958), Variations II (1961), Variations III (1963), Variations IV (1964)—are based on similar open-ended structural principles."

## 4. VARIATIONS V (1965) - OPERA CARDINE

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Setup tecnico e collaborazioni

**CITAZIONE 25 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 112-113):**
> "The first large step was the Cage-Cunningham Variations V, which was commissioned by the French-American Festival for a premiere at Philharmonic Hall at Lincoln Center in July 1965. [...] **Billy Klüver, Robert Moog, and others from Bell Laboratories built the special electronic music apparatus used in performance**. The elaborate lighting included film and slide projections designed by Stan VanDerBeek and additional images by Nam June Paik."

**CITAZIONE 26 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 113):**
> "For the Variations V project Cage enlisted the help of engineers from Bell Laboratories and the pioneering music synthesizer designer Robert Moog. **Cage approached Moog concerning the possibility of adapting the 'Terpsitone,' a special device that Leon Theremin had designed in the 1920s for use in activating musical sounds with dance**. The Terpsitone was a younger sibling to Theremin's more famous 'Thereminvox,' a capacitance-technology proximity-sensor from the late 1920s that produced solo melodic lines without physical touch."

**CITAZIONE 27 - Chadabe, *Electric Sound* (p. 83):**
> "It also included **twelve poles, actually modified theremins, built especially for the performance by Robert Moog and placed throughout the stage**. The idea was that the dancers would move within the sensitivity ranges of the poles, thereby triggering sounds. [...] There were also **photoelectric cells at the bases of the poles, which triggered additional sounds**."

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Interazione movimento-suono

**CITAZIONE 28 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 113):**
> "What was unique about Variations V is that **the choreographed movements of the dancers articulated the sound environment of the performance space**. The sound materials were 'selected' and activated by two sets of sensors: **the vertical capacitive antennas built by Moog** from designs originally conceived in the 1920s by Leon Theremin (his 'Terpsitone'), which responded to the locations of the dancers on the stage, and **a network of photoelectric cells** installed at the base of these antennas, which responded to changes in light intensity as the dancers moved past them."

**CITAZIONE 29 - Chadabe (citando Merce Cunningham):**
> "The general principle as far as I was concerned was **like the doors automatically opening when you enter a supermarket. The dancers triggered some of the sound possibilities, but the kind of sound, how long it might last, the possible repetition or delaying of it, was controlled by the musicians and technicians** who were at the numerous machines on a platform behind and above the dance space. They utilized tape machines, oscillators, and shortwave radios..."

**CITAZIONE 30 - Chadabe, *Electric Sound*:**
> "There were also objects—**a plant, a pillow, a pad, a table, and two chairs—to which contact microphones were attached and which, when moved or touched by the dancers, produced yet additional sounds**. Variations V contained a lot of ways to trigger sounds, and as if to put a period at the end of the sentence, Merce Cunningham, at the end of the dance, **rode a bicycle around the stage, its wheels wired for sound**."

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Complessità tecnica e portabilità

**CITAZIONE 31 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 171-172):**
> "At the premiere of Variations V in New York, **its extravagant wealth of complex and fragile equipment filled the massive stage and orchestra pit at Lincoln Center to overflowing**. Given its complexity, it is no wonder that there were technical problems resulting from inadequate installation and rehearsal. Nevertheless, the premiere was a huge theatrical coup, and Cunningham wanted to take it on tour."

**CITAZIONE 32 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 171):**
> "When I was engaged by the Cunningham Company in 1966, it was initially to provide the technological support for touring performances of Variations V. **Tudor and I stripped the technical equipment down to a workable touring package** for the 1966 European tour. Cage approved, advising us: '**Be practical**.' A major part of my contribution to its touring performances was just that—repairing fragile equipment and minimizing redundancy. [...] **The setup came to require only about six hours. Our 'virtuosity' really showed after the performance, when we could strike the musical parts of the set and load it into the shipping containers in less than two hours.**"

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Esperienza performativa

**CITAZIONE 33 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 113):**
> "Every production of the immensely complicated Variations V was logistically precarious, and no two people could then agree as to its artistic merits. For the audience **Variations V was like a multi-ring circus. For the performers it was participation in a machine environment**, chock full of images and gadgets: movies, TV images, slides, a bicycle and gym mat, plastic plants, pieces of furniture (all of them wired for direct sound by special microphones), and a garden of vertical antennas projecting upward from the floor."

**CITAZIONE 34 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 113):**
> "The complex interaction of the systems with the performers contained a measure of **technological unpredictability**. The audience rarely perceived a one-to-one correspondence between the dancers' movements and the sounds. Further, **differences of venue, lighting, and musical materials from one performance to another contributed to non-repeatability of results**. Because of these multi-leveled complexities, **Variations V had the unsettling effect of being both the same and different at each performance**."

## 5. CRISI DELLA TAPE MUSIC

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Coincidenza con la crisi del rituale concertistico

**CITAZIONE 35 - Bernardini, *Nuova Atlantide* (1985), citato in immagine project knowledge:**
> "**Come si può notare l'introduzione dell'elettronica dal vivo coincide (specialmente negli USA e in Giappone) con la crisi del rituale del concerto**, rituale che viene rivisto, modificato, distrutto, de-strutturato negli anni 60 praticamente per ogni brano."

**CITAZIONE 36 - Bernardini (continuazione):**
> "I fattori scatenanti di questa crisi sono molto articolati. Li riassumeremo come segue: **(1) l'enorme influenza di John Cage sui suoi contemporanei, paragonabile all'influenza della scuola di Darmstadt**; (2) **il progressivo indebolimento del linguaggio musicale** (ormai giunto ai minimi termini), che mette in crisi la legittimità del compositore, combinata con **l'assenza di una pratica strumentale radicata per quanto riguarda gli strumenti elettronici** (da qui nasce l'apertura a una partecipazione del pubblico molto più attiva); **l'improvvisazione libera**, il fatto compositivo in tempo reale che si fa carico quindi almeno del virtuosismo compositivo della 'sensibilità'); (3) **la permeabilità dei musicisti sperimentali** a una parteci-"

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Limitazioni della tape music

**CITAZIONE 37 - Chadabe, *Electric Sound* (p. 69):**
> "The use of tape with live performance, however, poses a question: Although the composer may have intended that the tape function as an accompanist, or as another performer, or even as an orchestra, **wasn't the tape machine insensitive and unyielding to a musician's fluctuations in tempo? And the answer is yes**."

**CITAZIONE 38 - Di Scipio, *Circuiti del tempo* [immagine p. 133]:**
> "Va comunque osservato che anche l'operatività professionale dei normali 'studi di registrazione' aveva implicazioni di un certo rilievo: sul lungo periodo, **la possibilità di registrare e montare il suono *in studio* influì profondamente sui modi di fare musica *dal vivo***, contribuendo al rinnovarsi delle pratiche performative strumentali e vocali."

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Figure chiave della sperimentazione live electronics

**CITAZIONE 39 - Bernardini, *Nuova Atlantide* (1985) [immagine]:**
> "Tra questi possiamo ricordare: **David Tudor**, assiduo collaboratore di Cage, con i suoi lavori *Fluorescent Sound*, nel quale amplifica e filtra le risonanze meccaniche di lampade al neon (1964), *Bandoneon! (Bandoneon fattoriale)* per circuiti audio, altoparlanti mobili, immagini video e impianto luci interamente attivati e controllati da un bandoneon argentino (1966), e **Rainforest** nel quale il materiale sonoro generato da oscillatori elettronici viene riprodotto non da altoparlanti convenzionali bensì da trasduttori 'occasionali', oggetti di metallo, legno o plastica che filtrano i segnali a seconda delle loro caratteristiche fisiche – un esempio di uso creativo degli altoparlanti (1968);"

**CITAZIONE 40 - Bernardini (continuazione):**
> "**David Behrman**, con *Runthrough*, una composizione il cui materiale sonoro, oscillatori, modulatori di frequenza e amplificatori controllati in tensione vengono inviati a una matrice di distribuzione dei suoni controllata da fotocellule: gli esecutori vengono muniti di piccole lampadine tascabili muovendo le quali interpretano la partitura (1968); **Robert Ashley**, con *The Wolfman* per voce amplificata e nastro, nel quale la voce viene deformata da un'amplificazione esagerata che, con effetti di *feedback*, raggiunge le desiderate dimensioni grottesche (1964)."

## 6. TRANSIZIONE CONCETTUALE

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Da concezione riproduttiva a produttiva

**CITAZIONE 41 - Di Scipio, *Circuiti del tempo* [immagine p. 133]:**
> "Gli 'studi di musica elettroacustica' furono infrastrutture di tipo alquanto diverso: furono pensati per sperimentare creativamente i mezzi tecnici stessi e quindi **per realizzare musiche *non* preesistenti all'operatività praticabile all'interno**. Vi era una differenza funzionale, che rifletteva però anche un diverso impegno tecnico e artistico, e che consisteva in un modo diverso di mettere in rapporto pensiero e prassi musicale: **a una concezione *riproduttiva* subentrava una concezione *produttiva***, nel senso generico di 'creativa' e 'inventiva', dove i mezzi diventavano essi stessi oggetto di elaborazione e potevano quindi essere progettati, riprogettati e configurati per assecondare specifiche esigenze compositive, piuttosto che per svolgere in modo ottimale compiti standardizzati (quali necessari ai fini della diffusione e del consumo di massa)."

**CITAZIONE 42 - Di Scipio, *Circuiti del tempo* [continuazione immagine p. 133]:**
> "I nuovi studi di musica elettroacustica si aprivano a un'attività sperimentale generalmente svincolata da schemi informati alle aspettative della circolazione commerciale, e invece **mirata a orientare le nuove tecniche verso nuove esigenze estetico-musicali**."

## 7. CONTESTO TECNOLOGICO

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Evoluzione della tecnologia elettronica

**CITAZIONE 45 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 169):**
> "Most significant to Cage's new work of this period was the rapid expansion of electronic musical technology in the early 1950s. By the late 1950s transistor technology had appeared, and by the early 1960s magnetic tape recording was entering general commercial distribution. Neither of these technological developments replaced the previous vacuum tube or disc-recording technologies, but they expanded the resources of creative artists such as Cage."

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Solid-state transistor technology

**CITAZIONE 46 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 20):**
> "By the late 1950s Cage had Tudor searching for a variety of electronic transducers, which were then becoming commonplace in the development of **solid-state transistor technology**."

**CITAZIONE 47 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 20-21):**
> "Electronic amplification and equalization were also used to project Cage's music from Atlas Eclipticalis and Winter Music in Cunningham's choreography Music Aeon (1964). This was done with **a rapidly burgeoning assortment of small equipment—transducers, preamplifiers, equalizers, and mixers. This equipment collection would soon become a space- and time-consuming menagerie** for Cage and Tudor."

**CITAZIONE 48 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 171):**
> "Cage approached Moog concerning the possibility of adapting the 'Terpsitone,' a special device that Leon Theremin had designed in the 1920s for use in activating musical sounds with dance. [...] At that time, **Moog was very busy in the early stages of designing and building his own electronic-music synthesizers**."

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Portabilità dell'equipment

Lanciato nel 1963 per iniziativa della Philips, il nastro a cassetta [...] **Più importanti risultarono le possibilità di fruizione legate alla portabilità e alla facilità di copia delle registrazioni**."

### PUNTO CHIAVE: Evoluzione dei sintetizzatori commerciali

## CITAZIONI AGGIUNTIVE - CONTESTO STORICO

### Tudor come mentore e innovatore

**CITAZIONE 55 - Mumma, *Cybersonic Arts* (p. 20):**
> "Working with Cage would provide an early wellspring for Tudor's own evolution from pianist-performer to composer with live electronics. **Tudor developed his own technical procedures for Cage's Variations II (1961), which extended those of Cartridge Music by enhancing the complex internal acoustical resonances of the piano**. Tudor often performed Variations II as a solo for piano with live electronics, notably at the 1965 ONCE Festival."

### Approccio "territory" di Cage

**CITAZIONE 56 - Chadabe, *Electric Sound* (p. 82-83):**
> "Cage's general approach was to **define a 'territory' and use all of the sounds within that territory as the sound material for a composition**. In Williams Mix, the territory was the world; **in Cartridge Music, it was sounds requiring amplification**; in Birdcage, it was birds and nature; in Roaratorio, it was Finnegans Wake."
